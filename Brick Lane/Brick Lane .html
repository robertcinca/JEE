Brick Lane


The heart of it all:  Brick Lane:  Food AND Religion AND business (meets history).
In answer to the eternal question:  "where are the best bagels in London (even the world)?”     Beigel Bake

One of the most important things to know about “Beigel Bake” is that it’s not the “Beigel Shop” which claims to be “Britain’s first and best.”
OK, try it for yourself!

Not only is Beigel Bake great for bagels, it’s also one of the best places to get a hot salt beef sandwich.  Hot salt beef is not the same as ‘corned beef’ in the United States.  It’s more like a boiled brisket. When it’s good it’s fabulous. When it’s lousy—you shouldn’t know from it.

Gherkins, on a hot salt beef sandwich. Get it on rye with mustard. 

Beigel Bake is, in fact, one of the best illustrations imaginable of one of the oldest Jewish jokes in the world.

A Jewish guy is shipwrecked and stranded for years on a desert island. 
When he's finally rescued he shows the sailors the camp he's made. 
‘That's where I sleep,’ he says.  ‘That's where I cook and eat.’ 
‘And those are the two synagogues.’ 
Why?  Every Jew knows, of course. 
You have to have the shul(synagogue) you go to—and the one you'd never step foot in.

In the East End, to this day, there's the bagel shop you go to—and the one you don't go to.  It's still a good joke, which especially befits this particular island.

What is this with the nutty spelling of “bagel”?  Why “beigel”?  “Baigel”?  “Baigle”?  “Beigle”?

 

Former Blooms Restaurant in the East End
People sometimes speak fondly of the former Blooms restaurant in the East End. 
They’re nuts.
It wasn’t very good and a ripoff.  OK, it was terrible.
When I went with my friend Lois in 1979, the waiter kept saying ‘would you like this?’, ‘would you like that?’—we had no idea we were being charged for everything.  The one in Golders Green also sucked.  (Yes, that’s my professional, academic opinion.)  One of the best restaurant reviews of all time is Jay Rayner’s diatribe from 2007: http://www.theguardian.com/lifeandstyle/2007/jul/01/foodanddrink.shopping1
That isn’t to say, though, there isn’t good Jewish food to be had in London. 

  • Should you be away from the East End, you can also find some worthwhile fressing (eating) on Oxford Street:  Selfridges.
  • Yes, Selfridges.  Most of the UK’s famous department stores, with the great exception of M&S, were not Jewish-founded or Jewish oriented in any way.  Selfridge’s is now known for “Mr Selfridge” played by the über-Jewish Jeremy Piven, but that doesn’t quite make Selfridge’s Jewish.  But they do have a very good salt beef bar, called the Brass Rail.  It’s slightly pricier than the others but the quality is consistently superb.
  • In the old days people used to joke about the poor quality and availability of coffee in the UK.  That was never really the case, and even less true now.  On Brick Lane there are a number of excellent places for coffee.  My favorite is “Full Stop.”

Gourmet Chocolate Shop
A relatively new addition to the rich offerings of Brick Lane is this gourmet chocolate shop.  It’s hard to miss at the southwestern corner of Brick Lane and Bacon Street.  The fare is good and interesting but a bit pricey (as to be expected.)

Jews are ever fearful of diabetes as a dreaded ‘Jewish’ disease but they love chocolate, as a rule. Sander Gilman, one of the most prolific and interesting scholars of all time, has ‘podcasted’ as well as written on the subject.

Amy Winehouse
Amy Winehouse (z''l) didn’t live in the East End, and identified mainly with Camden Town.  But her image is plastered all over the East End.   An exhibition devoted to Amy was staged at the Jewish Museum London in 2013:
http://www.jewishmuseum.org.uk/Amy

Is it Jewish?  Just hipster?  A little of both, possibly.   You need to visit Brick Lane during off-hours to see these.

How many Amy Winehouse images are there in the East End?  Who knows. This one is on the intersection with Bacon Street.
