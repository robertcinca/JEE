Liverpool Street Station


Probably the best place to begin a tour of the Jewish East End is Liverpool Street Station.  Here you have something new-ish, which is a reminder of a very important event.  At the southwest corner of the station is ‘Hope Square,’ featuring this sculpture where you can relax and plan your excursion. 

Image: Kindertransport Memorial
Well-put, I’d say (plaque at Hope Square).

Image: Kindertransport Memorial, Liverpool Street Station
In the wake of the pogroms of the 9th and 10th November 1938 in Nazi Germany, what is now termed the Reichskristallnacht, British citizens launched an extraordinary effort:  the Kindertransport.  Thousands of unaccompanied Jewish children were brought into Britain.  Around 2,500 of them were Protestant and Catholic, but classified as “Jewish” under the absurd Nazi race laws.   Liverpool Street Station, is, indeed,
an appropriate place for such a monument.

The best book on Kristallnacht is by Alan Steinweis:
http://www.hup.harvard.edu/catalog.php?isbn=9780674036239

 


Some people find the Kindertransport sculpture tacky, or kitschy.  I think its good and a really fitting memorial--and in the right place.
Liverpool Street Station remains a major hub for transport, and there are many sites of Jewish interest within a few minute stroll.

 


The points of embarkation, where the children of the Kindertransport left, are important to recall. Some might object to the litter on the sculpture.  It gets cleaned up. And it’s not a terrible thing.  Littering, after all, is an English trait.  ‘Hope Square’ is not pristine.

On the lower level of Liverpool Street Station, conveniently located at the “Meeting Point,” is another small sculpture.  This, too, recalls the Kindertransport as well as additional efforts to bring Jewish children to Britain in the face of the Nazi menace—the most important of which was the mission of Nicholas Winton.  Make sure to see both versions.

It’s not easy to see here but there’s a subtle ‘Star of David’ image in the clock face of Liverpool Street Station tower.  It’s probably a meaningless, generic symbol and I don’t know when it appeared there.  But some might read more into it.  There are a few others like it, mainly on churches, in the East End.

Star of David

  • The ‘star’ or ‘shield’ of David, supposedly a symbol from the time of the biblical King David, is now mainly regarded as a Jewish symbol.
  • As you can see on the Liverpool Street Station clock tower, and at least two other places in the East End, it was used as a generic symbol, as well, on religious as well as private and municipal buildings.  It also can be found in continental Europe.
  • The foundational historian of Jewish mysticism, Gershom Scholem, wrote about the largely non-Jewishly-specific history of the symbol.
  • Although it was used by numerous Jewish philanthropic and proto-nationalist organizations prior to 1897, after the Zionist Congress in Basel (now known as the First Zionist Congress), it mainly came to be associated with the Zionist Movement, as well as a generic Jewish representation.
  • More on this can be found in Michael Berkowitz, Zionist Culture and West European Jewry before the First World War (Cambridge University Press, 1993, and University of North Carolina Press, 1996).
