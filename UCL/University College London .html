University College London


Bloomsbury, though not often thought of for its Jewish dimensions, has a number of extremely significant sites of Jewish interest, not the least of which is University College London.  “The Godless College in Gower Street,” it was the first university in the UK to claim a secular perspective and admit Jews, Catholics, dissidents, and non-believers altogether.
www.ucl.ac.uk
Even prior to the emergence of ‘Jewish Studies’ in the United States and Europe, the field was pioneered at UCL by Hyman Hurwitz.
UCL was the first university in the world to encourage Jews and Judaism to be explored on their own terms, as opposed to subservient to Christianity (as at Oxbridge), especially by becoming the institutional home of the Jewish Historical Society of England. 
Despite being the “Godless College” there is one piece of quasi-religious material culture apparent in the Gustav Tuck Theatre:  the motto of the Jewish Historical Society of England, in Hebrew

Modern economics, that is, economics treated in an intellectualized, detached, abstract manner could was pioneered by David Ricardo and those inspired by him at UCL.

Ricardo didn’t remain a Jew after adulthood.  He became a Unitarian, but heck, we all know what they are.  (Great religion, really.)  But his criticism of Malthus, and the way that he asked questions regarding ‘political economy’ changed economics forever.
